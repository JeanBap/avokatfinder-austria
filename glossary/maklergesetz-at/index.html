Maklergesetz (Estate Agent Law)

Austria's Maklergesetz regulates estate agents, setting maximum commission rates, mandatory written agency agreements, and a two-sided commission structure where both buyer and seller may owe the agent.

What Is the Maklergesetz?

Austria's Maklergesetz (Federal Brokerage Act, BGBl 1996/262) governs the rights and obligations of professional intermediaries (Makler) including real estate agents. It sets: maximum commission rates (capped by regulation); the requirement for a written mandate before commission is owed; the conditions under which commission is earned; and the circumstances in which commission may be charged to buyer, seller, or both.

Commission Rates and Caps

The Real Estate Agents' Ordinance (Immobilienmaklerverordnung) caps commission at 3% of the purchase price for both buyer and seller (plus 20% VAT on the commission). In practice, agents often charge each party separately — so a buyer may pay up to 3% and the seller up to 3%. However, for residential properties sold for owner-occupation, regulations since 2023 restrict commission charged to the buyer: the ordering party (almost always the seller) bears the full commission, and agents cannot charge buyers who did not sign the mandate. This reform shifted cost significantly to sellers.

2023 Commission Reform

From 1 July 2023, a key reform of the Maklergesetz prevents agents from charging commission to buyers of residential properties when the seller ordered the agent's services. The Bestellerprinzip (ordering-party principle) now applies: whoever commissions the agent pays. This mirrors the German model implemented in 2021. In practice, agents now negotiate commission entirely with sellers, and buyers of new primary residences typically pay no agent commission.

Mandate Requirement

Commission is only owed if a written mandate (Maklervertrag) was in place before the agent introduced the buyer to the property. An oral agreement is insufficient — the agent cannot claim commission based on a verbal arrangement. The mandate must specify the property, the commission rate, and the mandate term. Buyers should avoid signing blank commission agreements and should read any documentation the agent presents before a viewing.

Commission Trigger

The agent earns commission when a legally binding contract is concluded between a buyer they introduced and the seller — not merely when the parties exchange correspondence. If the buyer introduced by Agent A ultimately buys through Agent B (or privately), Agent A may still claim commission if their introduction was the effective cause of the ultimate purchase. This requires careful documentation of who introduced whom and when.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Signing a buyer's commission agreement handed to you by the seller's agent at a viewing. Since the 2023 reform, buyers of residential property generally should not sign commission agreements.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Ask explicitly before any viewing: 'Did the seller commission you?' — if yes, the agent cannot legally charge you commission under the Bestellerprinzip reform.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the Bestellerprinzip apply to commercial property?

No — it applies to residential property (Wohnimmobilien) only. Commercial real estate follows the old double-commission model.

What if the agent refuses to show the property without a signed buyer's mandate?

This is technically unenforceable post-reform for residential property — the seller cannot prevent viewings by requiring buyer commission agreements.

Is 3% the actual market rate or just the cap?

3% is the cap. Effective commission is often negotiated below this, especially for higher-value properties or in a buyer's market.
