Austria Legal Term

Aufenthaltstitel

German: Aufenthaltstitel (Residence Permit)

Austrian residence permit for non-EU/EEA nationals allowing legal stay and work in Austria. Multiple categories exist including the Rot-Weiß-Rot Karte (Red-White-Red Card) for skilled workers and investors.

TL;DRAustrian residence permit for non-EU/EEA nationals allowing legal stay and work in Austria. Multiple categories exist including the Rot-Weiß-Rot Karte (Red-White-Red Card) for skilled workers and investors.

An Aufenthaltstitel is the formal Austrian residence permit granted to non-EU/EEA nationals authorising their stay in Austria beyond 90 days. Austria's residence permit system is administered by the Migration Authority (Amt der Landesregierung) in each province, with the Federal Migration Office (Bundesamt für Fremdenwesen und Asyl) handling more complex cases. EU/EEA citizens exercise free movement rights and do not need a formal Aufenthaltstitel.

The flagship residence permit for qualified migrants is the Rot-Weiß-Rot Karte (Red-White-Red Card — RWR Karte), a points-based system for skilled workers, self-employed persons, investors, and their family members. The RWR Karte is initially valid for 24 months and grants access to a specific employer (skilled worker card) or general labour market (self-employed/investor category). After 24 months, holders can apply for a Rot-Weiß-Rot Karte Plus, which allows unrestricted labour market access.

For property investors and wealthy individuals, the relevant category is the Niederlassungsbewilligung (settlement permit) or Aufenthaltsbewilligung (temporary residence). Austria does not operate a formal "golden visa" investment-to-residency programme like Spain, Portugal, or Greece. There is no direct route from property investment to residency — residency applications are assessed on general immigration criteria including proof of income, health insurance, and adequate accommodation.

EU citizens moving to Austria must register with the local Magistrat or Bezirksverwaltungsbehörde within four months of arrival if they plan to stay beyond three months. They receive an Anmeldebescheinigung (registration certificate) rather than a formal Aufenthaltstitel. Family members of EU citizens who are non-EU nationals receive an Aufenthaltskarte (residence card).

Language requirements: most Austrian residence permit categories for non-EU nationals require proof of German language skills at A1 or A2 level (depending on the permit type) before the permit is granted. For the RWR Karte for highly qualified workers, language requirements are waived for applicants scoring sufficient points on other criteria. The Austrian Integration Fund (ÖIF) administers approved language tests.

⚠ Common MistakeAssuming that purchasing Austrian property automatically creates a path to residency. Austria has no golden visa programme — property investment alone does not qualify you for an Aufenthaltstitel. Residency applications are assessed on employment, business, or income criteria independent of property ownership.
💡 Expert TipIf you are a non-EU national planning to move to Austria, apply for your Aufenthaltstitel from your home country's Austrian embassy or consulate — not from within Austria. Most non-EU nationals are not permitted to switch their immigration status from within Austria. The overseas application process typically takes 3–6 months.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I work in Austria on a tourist visa while my Aufenthaltstitel application is processed?

No — working in Austria without a valid work authorisation (which requires an Aufenthaltstitel that includes work permission) is illegal and can result in fines for both you and your employer, as well as affecting future permit applications.

What is the difference between the RWR Karte and the RWR Karte Plus?

The initial RWR Karte typically ties you to a specific employer or activity category and requires renewal after 24 months. The RWR Karte Plus (available after continuous legal residence, typically 12–24 months with the RWR Karte) grants unrestricted access to the Austrian labour market — you may work for any employer in any sector.

Does Austrian residency lead to citizenship?

Austrian citizenship by naturalisation requires 10 years of continuous legal residence, with reductions to 6 years for significant integration (language, civic knowledge, community engagement) or contributions to Austria. Dual citizenship is generally not permitted under Austrian law — most applicants must renounce their previous citizenship before naturalisation.
