Wohnungseigentumsgesetz

The Wohnungseigentumsgesetz (WEG) is Austria's Apartment Ownership Act, the primary legislation governing the creation, ownership, and management of condominium units (Wohnungseigentum) in multi-unit residential and commercial buildings.

Austria's Wohnungseigentumsgesetz (WEG 2002, as amended) establishes the legal framework for flat ownership (Wohnungseigentum) — the form of ownership in which a person holds exclusive ownership of a specific apartment, office, or car parking space, combined with a proportional co-ownership (Miteigentumsanteil) of the common parts of the building.

Under the WEG, each apartment owner holds a Mindestanteil (minimum share) in the entire property, calculated as the ratio of the unit's floor area to the total floor area of the building. This share is registered in the Grundbuch (land register) and determines the owner's voting weight in the owners' community and their contribution to common costs.

The Eigentümergemeinschaft (owners' community) is the legal entity representing all owners collectively. It enters contracts for building management, maintenance, and insurance. Decisions are made by majority vote at general meetings, with weightings based on Mindestanteile. Major decisions — sale of common areas, structural works — require qualified majorities or unanimity.

Each owner must contribute to the Rücklage (reserve fund) for major repairs and to the laufende Betriebskosten (operating costs) covering communal utilities, lift maintenance, cleaning, and building insurance. Contributions are proportional to the owner's Mindestanteil. Persistent non-payment creates an enforceable lien on the unit.

Buyers of Austrian apartments should review the Rücklage balance, upcoming capital works approved by the Eigentümergemeinschaft, and any outstanding disputes between owners. The Grundbuch extract will show all charges and restrictions on the unit. A property lawyer (Rechtsanwalt) or notary should review all documents before purchase.

⚠ Common MistakeBuyers sometimes overlook the Rücklage balance and scheduled major works. A building with a depleted reserve and an upcoming lift replacement or facade renovation will levy a Sonderumlage (special assessment) on all owners shortly after purchase.
💡 Expert TipRequest the Hausverwaltung's (building manager's) last three years of accounts and the current Rücklage balance. Also check the Eigentümergemeinschaft's meeting minutes for any adopted major works resolutions. These documents are your clearest indicator of near-term additional costs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the WEG the same as the Mietrechtsgesetz (MRG)?

No. The WEG governs ownership of individual units; the MRG governs rental relationships between landlords and tenants. Both may apply simultaneously if an owner rents out their WEG-owned apartment.

Can I use my apartment as I wish under WEG?

You have extensive rights within your unit. However, changes affecting common elements, the facade, or structural components require Eigentümergemeinschaft approval.

How do I check if there are unresolved disputes in the Eigentümergemeinschaft?

Request access to the Protokolle (minutes) of recent owners' meetings and ask the Hausverwaltung about any pending legal proceedings (Schlichtungsverfahren or court actions).
