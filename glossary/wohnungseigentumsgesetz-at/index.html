Austria Legal Term

Wohnungseigentumsgesetz

German: Wohnungseigentumsgesetz (WEG) — Condominium Law

Austria's law governing apartment ownership (Wohnungseigentum), where each owner holds a share in the whole building proportional to their apartment's value, combined with the exclusive right to use their unit.

TL;DRAustria's law governing apartment ownership (Wohnungseigentum), where each owner holds a share in the whole building proportional to their apartment's value, combined with the exclusive right to use their unit.

The Wohnungseigentumsgesetz (WEG 2002) is Austria's primary law governing condominium-style apartment ownership. Under Austrian law, a Wohnungseigentümer (apartment owner) does not hold title to their apartment as a separate parcel — instead, they own a fraction (Miteigentumsanteil) of the entire property combined with an exclusive use right (Nutzungsrecht) over their specific unit. This fraction is expressed as a share (Anteil) and recorded in the Grundbuch.

The WEG governs the relationship between co-owners: how costs are shared, how decisions are made, and what each owner may do with their unit and the common areas (allgemeine Teile). All structural elements — the building envelope, roof, load-bearing walls, lifts, shared heating systems, and communal gardens — are shared property. Each owner's financial obligation for common costs is proportional to their Miteigentumsanteil.

Management of Austrian apartment buildings under the WEG is typically handled by a professional Hausverwaltung (building management company) appointed by the majority of owners. The Hausverwaltung collects monthly Betriebskosten (running costs — includes insurance, maintenance, cleaning, and reserve contributions), administers the Rücklage (mandatory reserve fund), and organises the annual Eigentümerversammlung (owners' meeting).

Austria requires all Wohnungseigentum buildings to maintain a Rücklage (maintenance reserve fund). The Hausverwaltung sets the monthly contribution level based on the building's condition and anticipated maintenance needs. Insufficient reserves mean special assessments (Sonderumlage) — one-off additional charges for major works that all owners must pay. Before purchasing, check the Rücklage balance and any planned special works.

For expat buyers, the monthly Betriebskosten statement gives a clear picture of ongoing building costs. Request the last 12 months of Betriebskosten Abrechnungen (annual statements) and the Rücklage balance. Also check whether the building has an active Hausverwaltung contract — a building without professional management (a rare "selbstverwaltetes" building) carries higher administrative risk for non-resident owners.

A Viennese apartment building with a €0 reserve and a roof that needs replacing will issue a special assessment (Sonderumlage) shortly after you become an owner — a cost you should have discounted from your offer price.

Compare the reserve balance against the building's age and condition. Also request the last Eigentümerversammlung Protokoll (meeting minutes) to check for any planned major works.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I rent out my Austrian Wohnungseigentum apartment?

Generally yes — you have the right to use and let your unit as you wish, subject to any restrictions in the Nutzungsvereinbarung (use agreement) registered with the Grundbuch, and subject to Austrian tenancy law (MRG — Mietrechtsgesetz) which provides strong tenant protections for long-term rentals.

What majority is needed to approve major works in an Austrian WEG building?

Ordinary maintenance is decided by simple majority of Miteigentumsanteile. Major renovation works require a qualified majority (more than half of all shares). Works that significantly change the building's character require unanimity or court approval. Emergency repairs can be authorised by the Hausverwaltung without a vote.

What happens to outstanding Betriebskosten if I buy from a seller who has arrears?

Betriebskosten arrears in Austrian WEG buildings are a personal debt of the seller, not a charge on the property itself. However, the Hausverwaltung may pursue the new owner in certain circumstances. Request a written confirmation from the Hausverwaltung that the seller's account is clear before completing your purchase.
