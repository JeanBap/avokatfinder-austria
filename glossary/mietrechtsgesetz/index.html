Austria Legal Term

Mietrechtsgesetz (MRG)

German: Mietrechtsgesetz — Austrian Tenancy Law

Austria's principal tenancy law providing strong protections for residential tenants including rent controls (Richtwertmiete), restrictions on eviction, and rights of lease succession. Applies to most older Austrian rental properties.

TL;DRAustria's principal tenancy law providing strong protections for residential tenants including rent controls (Richtwertmiete), restrictions on eviction, and rights of lease succession. Applies to most older Austrian rental properties.

The Mietrechtsgesetz (MRG) is Austria's primary residential tenancy law, providing notably strong tenant protections compared to most European jurisdictions. The MRG applies to most apartments in residential buildings built before 1945 (and many later buildings), controlling: maximum permitted rents (Richtwertmiete system), the landlord's grounds for terminating a lease, the tenant's right to sublet and pass on the tenancy, and minimum standards for the condition of the property.

The Richtwertmiete (benchmark rent) system sets maximum rents per square metre for MRG-regulated properties, varying by province (Bundesland). In Vienna, the 2024 Richtwert is approximately €6.15/m² net, to which additions or deductions are made based on location, equipment, condition, and storey. Charging above the permitted level (Überschreitung des Richtwertes) gives tenants the right to claim back overpaid rent for up to three years retroactively.

MRG tenants have extensive security of tenure. Landlords can only terminate a tenancy on specific statutory grounds (kündigungsgeschützte Miete): non-payment of rent, material breach, the tenant's own vacating, or the landlord's pressing personal need (wichtiger Eigenbedarfsgrund) — the last being difficult to prove and subject to compensation obligations. Month-to-month termination without reason, common in other European countries, is not permitted for MRG-protected tenancies.

The MRG also grants tenants the right to pass on their tenancy to close family members living in the apartment (Eintrittsrecht) — effectively a tenant succession right that can extend a tenancy indefinitely through generations. This right significantly affects the value of MRG-regulated rental properties, as landlords cannot freely choose their tenants.

Not all Austrian rental properties are fully MRG-regulated. Single-family houses, newly constructed buildings (since 1945 in some categories), and properties rented as second homes or holiday accommodation are partially or fully exempt. The full MRG/partial MRG/non-MRG status of a property you are purchasing as an investment is critical to understand — it determines your rental income potential, ability to raise rents, and options for vacant possession.

⚠ Common MistakePurchasing an Austrian investment property without determining its MRG status. A fully MRG-regulated property with a long-term tenant paying Richtwertmiete may yield 30–50% below market rent, with no practical mechanism for vacant possession. This dramatically affects investment returns and must be priced into any offer.
💡 Expert TipBefore purchasing any Austrian rental property as an investment, obtain a legal opinion on the property's MRG status from an Austrian Rechtsanwalt. The difference between full MRG, partial MRG, and MRG-exempt status determines your rental income ceiling, eviction rights, and ability to set market rents — the most commercially significant legal factor in Austrian residential investment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I increase the rent on an MRG-regulated apartment?

Increases are limited to annual indexation based on the Consumer Price Index (Verbraucherpreisindex) and adjustments for improvements to the property's category. Free market rent increases are not possible under the full MRG.

Does the MRG apply to short-term furnished rentals?

Furnished apartments rented for defined short periods (under 6 months typically) may qualify for partial MRG exemption. Holiday rentals are generally exempt. The exact threshold is fact-specific — consult an Austrian tenancy lawyer before structuring a short-term rental arrangement to avoid inadvertent MRG coverage.

What happens to an MRG tenancy when I sell the property?

The tenancy transfers automatically to the new owner with all existing rights intact. The new owner cannot terminate the tenancy simply because they have purchased the property. Any sitting tenant with MRG protection remains, and their terms continue unchanged — purchase does not reset the tenancy relationship.
