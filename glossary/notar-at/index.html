What is Notar (Notary Public)?

Licensed legal professional in Austria who authenticates documents, certifies signatures, conducts real estate transactions, and provides legal advisory services with official authority.

Key Facts — Notar (Notary Public)
  • Licensed legal professional appointed by Provincial Governor and organized into regional chambers
  • Conducts real estate transactions with official authentication authority (Beurkundung)
  • Authenticates documents, certifies signatures, and drafts wills, trusts, and corporate documents
  • Acts as impartial representative of the transaction, not advocate for either buyer or seller
  • Notary fees regulated by statute and scale from €2,500–€4,500 for typical property transactions

The Notar (Notary) in Austria is a highly regulated legal professional distinct from attorneys (Rechtsanwälte) who combines notarial functions with legal advisory authority. Austrian Notare are appointed by the Provincial Governor (Landeshauptmann) and are organized into regional chambers (Notariatskammern). They serve as impartial authenticators of documents, certify signatures, record wills and trusts, and conduct real estate transactions with official authority. The role is among Europe's oldest legal professions, dating back centuries, and Austrian Notare maintain strict professional standards, liability insurance, and ethical obligations.

In real estate transactions, the Notar plays a central coordinating role. They prepare the property purchase agreement (Kaufvertrag), explain the legal consequences to all parties, facilitate payment arrangements, conduct anti-money-laundering verification, and ensure compliance with all statutory requirements. The Notar must verify that the seller has clear title, that financing conditions are met, and that all parties understand their obligations. The Notar acts as an impartial representative of the transaction itself, not advocate for either party, though they provide legal guidance to ensure fairness. After the sale is finalized, the Notar submits the transaction documentation to the district court for Grundbuch registration.

Notare authenticate documents through multiple legal mechanisms. Official authentication (Beurkundung) gives documents increased evidentiary weight in disputes and creates a presumption of authenticity. Certification of signatures (Beglaubigung) confirms that a person with known identity signed a document. Notare also draft and authenticate wills, powers of attorney, marriage agreements, and corporate documents. Their role in testamentary matters is particularly important; an authenticated will (Notariatsurkunde) avoids probate complications and ensures compliance with formal requirements. The Notar's official seal and signature make documents enforceable throughout Austria and across most European jurisdictions.

For expats, Notare provide essential guidance on Austrian legal requirements and international implications of transactions. They ensure that non-Austrian buyers understand property ownership restrictions (Foreign Property Act), that financing complies with Austrian banking law, and that tax obligations are clearly stated. Notare often coordinate with foreign lawyers to ensure international transactions comply with both Austrian and foreign law. This is particularly important for non-resident foreign buyers subject to special restrictions and reporting requirements. The Notar ensures that all parties have equal information and that the transaction is conducted fairly.

Costs for Notar services are regulated by the Notariatstaxtaxe (Notary Fee Schedule) and scale proportionally with transaction amounts. For a €500,000 property purchase, the Notar fee typically ranges from €2,500 to €4,500 depending on the specific services rendered. These fees are transparent and non-negotiable, though the Notar may offer fee reductions in some circumstances. The fee provides comprehensive services including legal advice, document preparation, authentication, and submission for Grundbuch registration. Most property buyers find the Notar's professional oversight well worth the cost, as it protects their investment and ensures legal compliance.

Common Mistake: Assuming that a Notar is like an attorney and hiring them to represent your interests against the other party. The Notar's role is to ensure fairness and legal compliance for all parties, not to advocate for one side. If you need legal representation in a dispute with the seller or third parties, you must hire a separate attorney (Rechtsanwalt). Use the Notar for transaction authentication and the attorney for adversarial legal matters.
Expert Tip: Interview the Notar before engaging them, especially if you're an expat unfamiliar with Austrian law. Ask about their experience with international buyers, their familiarity with your home country's legal system, and whether they can explain the transaction and legal implications in English. Some Notare specialize in international transactions and may coordinate with your home country's legal professionals to ensure compliance with both systems.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to hire a separate attorney if I use a Notar for my real estate purchase?

The Notar provides legal guidance and authentication during the transaction itself. However, if you have concerns about the terms, suspect title issues, or need representation in disputes, you may benefit from consulting an attorney independently. Many buyers consult attorneys before signing and rely on the Notar to authenticate the final agreement. For complex transactions or non-standard conditions, attorney consultation is advisable.

Can a Notar help me if I'm purchasing property remotely as a non-resident foreigner?

Yes, Notare regularly handle international transactions with non-resident buyers. They can conduct video consultations, arrange for remote signing (with proper authentication), and coordinate with your foreign attorney. However, the Notar cannot provide tax or immigration advice—those require specialized advisors. The Notar ensures the purchase complies with Austrian real estate law and the Foreign Property Act.

What happens if I disagree with the Notar's interpretation of the contract?

The Notar is required to explain the legal consequences of the transaction to all parties. If you disagree, the Notar will note your objection (Einspruch) and may seek clarification. However, if you fundamentally disagree with the transaction terms, you should withdraw before signing. Once the Notar authenticates the agreement, it becomes a legally binding document. Consult an attorney before signing if you have significant reservations.
