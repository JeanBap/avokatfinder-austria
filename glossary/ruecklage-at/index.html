Rücklage

The Rücklage is the mandatory building reserve fund in Austrian condominium (Wohnungseigentum) buildings, collected monthly from all owners to finance major repairs and renovation of common parts of the building.

Under the Austrian Wohnungseigentumsgesetz (WEG 2002), every Eigentümergemeinschaft (owners' community) in a condominium building must maintain a Rücklage — a dedicated reserve fund held in a separate bank account. The fund's purpose is to accumulate capital for major maintenance works that cannot be funded from routine operating income alone.

Monthly contributions to the Rücklage are set by the Hausverwalter (building manager) in the annual budget, subject to approval by the owners' assembly. The WEG prescribes that contributions must be set at a 'reasonable' level — in practice this means reflecting the building's age, condition, and expected future expenditure. Older buildings typically require higher contributions.

The Rücklage may only be used for capital works — structural repairs, roof replacement, lift overhaul, facade renovation, and similar major expenditure. It may not be used for routine operating costs such as communal electricity, cleaning, or minor maintenance. Any withdrawal requires a decision of the Eigentümergemeinschaft or, for urgent works, authorisation by the Hausverwalter under their delegated authority.

Owners who fail to pay their Rücklage contributions create a debt enforceable by the Eigentümergemeinschaft, including registration of a lien (Pfandrecht) against the apartment in the Grundbuch. Persistent non-payers can be excluded from voting rights on fund-related decisions under specific conditions.

Buyers of Austrian apartments should review the Rücklage balance and the Hausverwaltung's multi-year maintenance plan (langfristiger Instandhaltungsplan) before purchase. A depleted fund combined with an upcoming Sonderumlage (special levy) for a roof replacement or lift modernisation represents a significant near-term cost that a buyer will inherit.

⚠ Common MistakeBuyers focus on monthly Betriebskosten (operating costs) and overlook the Rücklage balance. A building with a low reserve and a scheduled Sonderumlage for a lift replacement or roof renovation can cost new owners thousands of euros within months of purchase.
💡 Expert TipRequest the Hausverwaltung's last three years of Jahresabrechnungen (annual statements) and the current Rücklage balance. Also ask for the minutes of the last owners' assembly to identify any adopted major works resolutions or pending Sonderumlagen.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I withdraw my proportional Rücklage share when selling?

No. The Rücklage belongs to the Eigentümergemeinschaft, not individual owners. A seller cannot extract their share; it remains with the building and benefits the new buyer.

What happens if the Rücklage is insufficient for urgent work?

The Hausverwalter can levy a Sonderumlage on all owners to cover the shortfall, or arrange a building loan (Gemeinschaftsdarlehen) repaid via increased monthly contributions.

Is the Rücklage the same as the monthly Hausgeld?

No. Hausgeld covers routine operating costs (Betriebskosten). The Rücklage is a separate reserve for capital expenditure. Both are typically itemised separately in the monthly statement.
