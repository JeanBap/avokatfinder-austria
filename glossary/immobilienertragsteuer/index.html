Austria Legal Term

Immobilienertragsteuer

German: Immobilienertragsteuer (ImmoESt)

Austrian capital gains tax of 30% on profits from selling real estate. Key exemptions: primary residence (Hauptwohnsitzbefreiung) if lived in for 2 of the last 5 years, and self-built owner-occupied homes.

TL;DRAustrian capital gains tax of 30% on profits from selling real estate. Key exemptions: primary residence (Hauptwohnsitzbefreiung) if lived in for 2 of the last 5 years, and self-built owner-occupied homes.

The Immobilienertragsteuer (ImmoESt) is the Austrian capital gains tax applied to profits from selling real estate, introduced in 2012. The flat rate is 30% on the net gain (sale price minus acquisition cost, including purchase costs and documented improvements). This tax applies to all Austrian property sales regardless of how long the property has been held — there is no long-term holding period reduction in Austria.

The most important exemption is the Hauptwohnsitzbefreiung (primary residence exemption): if the seller has used the property as their primary residence (Hauptwohnsitz — registered in the Melderegister) for at least two of the five years immediately preceding the sale, the entire gain is exempt. Additionally, sellers who have continuously lived in the property as their primary residence from the date of construction or purchase are fully exempt regardless of duration.

Self-built (hergestellt) owner-occupied homes are also exempt: if the seller built the home themselves and used it as their primary residence, the exemption applies even if the two-of-five-years condition is not fully met. The exemption covers the land and the building in this case.

For non-resident sellers, the ImmoESt mechanism works differently: the buyer's notary or lawyer is required to withhold ImmoESt from the purchase price and remit it directly to the Austrian tax authority. The withholding rate is 30% of the gain (for properties acquired after 2002) or 4.2% of the sale price (for pre-2002 "old" properties using the simplified Altvermögen calculation). Non-resident sellers can file an Austrian tax return to claim a refund if the actual tax is lower than the amount withheld.

For expat sellers who have not used the property as their primary residence (e.g. Austrian holiday homes or buy-to-let investments), the full 30% ImmoESt applies. Austria's double tax treaties may allow credit for ImmoESt against tax due in the seller's country of residence — but this requires active filing in both countries. Keep all purchase receipts and improvement invoices to maximise the deductible acquisition cost and minimise the taxable gain.

⚠ Common MistakeSelling an Austrian holiday home or rental property without retaining all purchase invoices and improvement receipts. The ImmoESt base is sale price minus all documented acquisition costs — missing receipts inflate your taxable gain and your 30% tax bill. Keep all records from the day of purchase.
💡 Expert TipIf you have used your Austrian property as your primary residence for at least two years in the five years before sale, you qualify for the full Hauptwohnsitzbefreiung — a complete exemption. Plan your sale timeline with this window in mind: moving out of a property triggers a five-year countdown during which you must sell to claim the exemption.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does ImmoESt apply to gifts and inheritances of Austrian property?

Gifts and inheritances are not subject to ImmoESt at the time of transfer. However, when the recipient later sells the property, ImmoESt is calculated using the original acquisition cost of the giver/deceased — not the value at the time of the gift/inheritance. This means embedded gains can transfer along with the property.

Can I offset ImmoESt losses against gains from other Austrian property sales?

Yes — losses from Austrian property sales can be offset against gains from other Austrian real estate sales in the same tax year, reducing the overall ImmoESt liability. However, real estate losses cannot be offset against other income types (employment, business income).

Is there a double tax treaty issue if I am not Austrian resident?

Most of Austria's tax treaties allocate the right to tax real estate gains to the country where the property is located — meaning Austria can tax the gain regardless of where you live. You may then be entitled to credit in your country of residence for the Austrian ImmoESt paid, but this must be actively claimed through the treaty mechanism.
